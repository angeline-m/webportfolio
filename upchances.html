Project Experience

Using a PSD mockup, I had to create two pages for a fictional company based on the design that was given. The mockup for both the home and form pages were given to us in three sizes for responsive styling. This project required me to use everything I learned about web development up to that point: flexboxes, responsive styling, position values, the use of icons and SVG's, and responsive images.

Working on the project, I noticed a couple of typos on the mockup. When I double-checked with my instructor, she pointed out that the typos were intentional: they were put in there to see if the developer would notice and call it out. Typos, while not intentional, are commonplace in the real world. The designer may oversee small inconspicuous errors such as a misspelling. As unobtrusive as those mistakes are, we do not want to deliver clients products with any flaws. Thanks to my detailed eye and willingness to speak about it, I was the only one who mentioned the mistakes and was given the green light to correct them on the final product.

A challenge I had on this project was getting the fixed navigation menu shadow to show up as shown in the mockup. I spent a long time working with CSS styling to get the box-shadow to work to no avail. When I finally talked to my instructor about it, she gave me a quick fix that wasn't 100% perfect or identical, but worked well enough for the issue to become discreet. She mentioned that had I not said anything, she wouldn't have noticed. This taught me that, despite my great attention to small details, I can get lost in trying to make miniscule things work. Simply talking to the client can save oneself time and frustration (of course I'll always ensure that I've exhausted all my options first).

Secondly, this issue also taught me that I do not have to follow the mockup down to the last pixel. As someone who has been on both sides of a mockup (the one creating and the one receiving and following it), this can be hard to follow. I'm much more lenient with myself now because I understand that nobody is ever going to measure things down to the pixel to see if you've followed the design precisely. My perfectionist tendencies will still persist, but I've become more forgiving over time.
